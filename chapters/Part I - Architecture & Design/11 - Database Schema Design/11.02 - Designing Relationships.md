# 11.02 - Designing Relationships

> Relationships answer one question:
>
> **"How do business entities interact with each other?"**
>
> Tables are important, but relationships are what give a database meaning.

---

# Objective

In the previous chapter, we identified the business entities.

```
Clinic

Doctor

Patient

Appointment

Consultation

Diagnosis

Prescription

Medicine

Lab Test
```

These entities exist independently.

Now we need to answer an important question.

> **How are these entities connected?**

A backend engineer does not create relationships because the database supports foreign keys.

Relationships exist because the **business requires them**.

---

# Thinking Like a Backend Engineer

Never ask

> Which foreign key should I add?

Instead ask

> Which business object needs to know about another business object?

Example

A doctor works in a clinic.

Naturally,

```
Doctor

↓

Clinic
```

becomes a relationship.

The database is simply recording a business fact.

---

# Rule of Relationship Design

Every relationship should answer a real business question.

If you cannot explain why two entities are connected,

they probably should not be.

---

# Relationship 1

## Clinic → Doctor

```
Clinic

1

↓

∞

Doctor
```

### Business Meaning

A clinic employs multiple doctors.

Every doctor works under a clinic.

---

### Why does this relationship exist?

Without this relationship we cannot answer

- Which doctors work in Clinic A?
- How many doctors does a clinic have?
- Which clinic does this doctor belong to?

These are everyday business questions.

---

### Who Owns the Relationship?

The clinic.

Doctors are assigned to clinics.

---

### Alternative Design

```
Doctor

↓

Clinic
```

or

```
Clinic

↓

Doctors
```

Both describe the same business.

In relational databases,

the foreign key is stored on the **many** side.

Therefore,

```
Doctor

clinicId
```

---

### Trade-off

Current assumption

```
One Doctor

↓

One Clinic
```

If the business later allows

```
One Doctor

↓

Many Clinics
```

the relationship becomes

```
DoctorClinic
```

(Junction Table)

Architecture follows business requirements.

---

# Engineering Note

Never design for future possibilities that do not exist today.

Design for today's business.

Refactor when the business changes.

---

# Relationship 2

## Clinic → Patient

```
Clinic

1

↓

∞

Patient
```

### Business Meaning

Patients register with a clinic.

The clinic owns their medical records.

---

### Why?

Without Clinic → Patient

we cannot answer

- Patients registered in this clinic
- Total patients
- Clinic reports
- Clinic statistics

---

### Alternative

Some hospital systems share patients across branches.

In that case,

```
Patient

∞

↓

∞

Clinic
```

would require a junction table.

For our clinic,

registration belongs to one clinic.

---

# Relationship 3

## Doctor → Appointment

```
Doctor

1

↓

∞

Appointment
```

### Business Meaning

Appointments are booked with a specific doctor.

---

### Why?

Without this relationship

- Today's appointments
- Doctor availability
- Doctor schedule

cannot be calculated.

---

### Questions the Business Asks

```
Show Dr. Sharma's appointments today.

Show available slots.

Count completed consultations.
```

Every question requires

```
doctorId
```

---

# Relationship 4

## Patient → Appointment

```
Patient

1

↓

∞

Appointment
```

### Business Meaning

Patients visit multiple times.

Every visit creates another appointment.

---

### Why?

Medical history depends on repeated visits.

Without appointments,

the clinic cannot know

- first visit
- follow-up visits
- missed appointments
- cancellations

---

### Engineering Note

Appointments are scheduling records.

They are **not**

medical records.

This distinction becomes important later.

---

# Relationship 5

## Appointment → Consultation

```
Appointment

1

↓

1

Consultation
```

### Business Meaning

A consultation happens because an appointment occurred.

---

### Why not

```
Patient

↓

Consultation
```

directly?

Because every consultation originates from an appointment.

Appointments answer

"When?"

Consultations answer

"What happened?"

Both represent different business concepts.

---

### Why One-to-One?

One appointment results in one consultation.

If consultation never happened,

the appointment simply remains incomplete.

---

### Business Questions

- Did the consultation happen?
- Which appointment produced it?
- Which appointments remain pending?

---

# Relationship 6

## Consultation → Diagnosis

```
Consultation

1

↓

∞

Diagnosis
```

### Business Meaning

A consultation may produce one or multiple diagnoses.

Example

```
Hypertension

+

Diabetes
```

Both belong to the same consultation.

---

### Why separate Diagnosis?

Diagnosis is a medical conclusion.

Keeping it separate allows

- multiple diagnoses
- future coding systems
- reporting
- analytics

---

# Relationship 7

## Consultation → Prescription

```
Consultation

1

↓

∞

Prescription
```

### Business Meaning

Doctors prescribe treatment during consultations.

The prescription belongs to the consultation,

not directly to the patient.

---

### Why?

Imagine viewing patient history.

We should see

```
Consultation

↓

Prescription
```

instead of

```
Patient

↓

Prescription
```

The consultation explains **why** the medicine was prescribed.

---

# Relationship 8

## Prescription → Medicine

This relationship is different.

A prescription contains multiple medicines.

A medicine appears in many prescriptions.

```
Prescription

∞

↓

∞

Medicine
```

---

This is a

```
Many-to-Many
```

relationship.

---

# Why Many-to-Many?

Example

Prescription

```
Paracetamol

Vitamin C

Antibiotic
```

Another prescription

```
Paracetamol

Ibuprofen
```

The same medicine appears repeatedly.

---

### Solution

Create a junction table.

```
Prescription

↓

PrescriptionItem

↓

Medicine
```

---

### Why not store medicines as JSON?

Example

```
[
    "Paracetamol",
    "Vitamin C"
]
```

Problems

- Cannot search efficiently
- Cannot reuse medicines
- No foreign keys
- Difficult reporting
- Difficult analytics

Normalization solves these issues.

---

# Relationship 9

## Consultation → Lab Test

```
Consultation

1

↓

∞

Lab Test Recommendation
```

### Business Meaning

Doctors recommend investigations during consultations.

Examples

```
CBC

MRI

X-Ray

Blood Sugar
```

Recommendations belong to the consultation.

Not to the patient directly.

---

# Relationships We Intentionally Avoid

Good design also means saying **No**.

---

## Clinic → Medicine

```
Clinic

↓

Medicine
```

No relationship.

Medicines are universal.

Clinics prescribe them.

They do not own them.

---

## Doctor → Medicine

Doctors prescribe medicines.

They do not own medicine records.

No direct relationship.

---

## Patient → Medicine

Patients receive medicines.

They do not own medicines.

The relationship already exists through

```
Patient

↓

Consultation

↓

Prescription

↓

Medicine
```

Avoid duplicate relationships.

---

# Engineering Note

If information can already be reached through an existing relationship,

avoid creating another shortcut relationship.

Duplicate relationships often create inconsistent data.

---

# Relationship Summary

| Relationship                | Type         | Business Reason                              |
| --------------------------- | ------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| Clinic → Doctor             | One-to-Many  | Clinic employs doctors                       |
| Clinic → Patient            | One-to-Many  | Clinic registers patients                    |
| Doctor → Appointment        | One-to-Many  | Doctor receives appointments                 |
| Patient → Appointment       | One-to-Many  | Patient books appointments                   |
| Appointment → Consultation  | One-to-One   | Consultation is the result of an appointment |
| Consultation → Diagnosis    | One-to-Many  | Multiple diagnoses are possible              |
| Consultation → Prescription | One-to-Many  | Treatment belongs to consultation            |
| Prescription ↔ Medicine     | Many-to-Many | One prescription contains many medicines     |
| Consultation → Lab Test     | One-to-Many  | Doctor recommends multiple investigations    |

---

# How a Senior Engineer Reviews Relationships

Before approving a schema,

ask

- Why does this relationship exist?
- Which business question does it answer?
- Can this relationship be derived from another one?
- Is this relationship duplicated?
- Does this reflect today's business or a future assumption?
- What breaks if this relationship is removed?

If the answer is unclear,

the relationship probably needs redesign.

---

# Common Mistakes

## Creating Relationships Without Business Meaning

Wrong

```
Doctor

↓

Medicine
```

because "it might be useful."

Relationships should solve business problems.

---

## Designing Everything as Many-to-Many

Many-to-Many relationships increase complexity.

Only use them when the business truly requires them.

---

## Duplicating Information

Example

```
Patient

↓

Prescription
```

and

```
Patient

↓

Consultation

↓

Prescription
```

One of these is unnecessary.

Keep one source of truth.

---

## Optimizing Too Early

Do not create relationships just because they might improve queries.

Correctness comes before optimization.

Performance can be improved later with indexes.

---

# Summary

Relationships describe how business entities interact.

They should always originate from real business requirements, not from database features or ORM capabilities.

A well-designed relationship answers a business question, has a clear owner, avoids duplication, and keeps the domain easy to understand.

Once the relationships are finalized, the database structure almost designs itself.
